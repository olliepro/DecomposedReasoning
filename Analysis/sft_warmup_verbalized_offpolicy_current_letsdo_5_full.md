Amides are significantly weaker bases than corresponding amines due to the following key factors:

1. **Resonance Stabilization**:
   In amides (RCONH₂), the lone pair on the nitrogen is delocalized into the adjacent carbonyl group (C=O) via resonance. This delocalization forms a resonance structure where the negative charge is stabilized on the highly electronegative oxygen atom:
   \[
   \text{R-C(=O)-NH}_2 \leftrightarrow \text{R-C(-O^-)=NH}_2^+
   \]
   The nitrogen’s lone pair is thus less available to accept a proton because it is already involved in stabilizing the molecule through resonance. In contrast, amines (RNH₂) lack this delocalization, so their nitrogen lone pairs remain localized and more available for protonation.

2. **Inductive Effect**:
   The carbonyl group in amides is strongly electron-withdrawing due to its polarized double bond (C=O). This inductive effect pulls electron density away from the nitrogen, reducing its electron richness and making it less nucleophilic/basic. Amines, lacking an adjacent electron-withdrawing group, retain higher electron density on their nitrogen atoms.

3. **Destabilization of Protonated Form**:
   When an amine accepts a proton, it forms a stable alkylaminium ion (e.g., RNH₃⁺), which is well-solvated in water. For an amide, protonation would place a positive charge on the nitrogen adjacent to the electron-withdrawing carbonyl group. This destabilizes the resulting cation because the carbonyl’s inductive effect exacerbates the positive charge, and resonance cannot effectively delocalize the charge (as the nitrogen is already part of the stabilized resonance structure in the neutral amide).

**Conclusion**: The combined effects of resonance delocalization, electron-withdrawing inductive effects, and destabilization of the protonated state make amides far less basic than amines. This explains why amides do not dissolve in dilute acids, whereas amines readily form water-soluble salts.
